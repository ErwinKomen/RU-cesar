01. The call of the hoopoe

‎‎I am flying with my arms stretched out and looking at our village below, the street where we live, my friends playing football at the square, my grandpa's brother – we call him Great Father – mowing with a scythe, young women returning from the stream, and my grandma, pulling up weeds among the onions in the yard.

I call out, “‎‎Hoop-hoop-hoop! Can you see me? I am FLYING!”

“Look, look at him! How did he manage to get up in the air?” My friends and neighbours are all amazed. My friends are jealous, it is visible on their faces, especially Bakar. He is always quarrelling with me. He just cannot stand it when I am better at school or in a game.

I am laughing for joy. “Hoop-hoop-hoop! Look here, I am up in the air!”

Then suddenly, I hear mum’s voice, “Dakaz, wake up! Hurry up! We really have to get going.”

I am startled and the nice dream evaporates as mum shakes me. I sit up straight rubbing my eyes. It has become completely light outside, though the sun is only starting to rise. The beautiful summer morning is hurrying along. Mum and dad are also. Mum tells me to get ready, and runs off to get some errands done. Daddy is busy harnessing the horse in the courtyard. He has just put the collar over the horse's neck and is about to push the shafts through the togs to fasten them to the harness. The bag hanging over the horse's mouth has a bullet in it that serves as a weight. He snorts as he enjoys her barley. I walk up to dad, wanting to help him, but I am still quite sleepy. Dad tells me, “Dakaz, you don’t seem completely awake, yet. Hurry up and go wash yourself, put on some old clothes and then come back here! Hurry up! We're running late, there's a long road ahead of us.”

I rush inside. If dad gets angry, he will not take me with him and I do not want to miss out on going to mow the field.

The year is 1965. ‎‎Fortunately, school is over and we are home for the summer holidays. I have passed to grade six, but for the moment, I cannot think of school. There is still more than a month left before school starts again. Vacation is so much fun! We can play ball until we get bored. We go swimming in the Argun River. We climb the mulberry trees and have a ball eating the sweet purple berries and talking about movies. We play hide-and-seek or warrior-warrior in the evening. There are so many great things to do!

‎‎Every year mum goes into alarm mode. She wants to help dad prepare the hay for the winter, but dad argues that it is not a woman’s job, however the task cannot be accomplished without her. He knows he needs her for cutting and binding the hay and she learnt from early childhood how to use the scythe. ‎‎She mastered these things when lived in Siberia. ‎‎Having been taken out of their homeland, during the time of the deportation, Chechens met all kinds of difficulties like hunger, cold, violence and oppression by the government. “May no Muslim ever experience such a harsh time again!” says mum, whenever she remembers that time. ‎‎Since mum's parents were already old and frail when she was small she had to do all kinds of outside work, such as preparing wood and hay, and tending the cattle, from a young age.

‎‎Our horse walks on the soft cart road at a steady pace. The road winds through the growing field, which is part of the communal farm. I nudge closer to my dad, sitting on the wooden plank at the front end of the cart.

“Dad, please let me steer Chonker.”

“Okay, come up here and take the reins.”

‎‎The horse walks very smoothly, as if it gets energy from the fresh air in the plains. Every so often, I look back at mum with a big smile, happy that I have been given such a huge responsibility. I am now the driver of the cart! Mum understands me well. She smiles back at me as she holds the food steady so it does not spill out of the jars and bowls.

“Go slowly, Dakaz! At this speed the milk will churn.”

I rein the horse in just a little bit, and it slows down its gait. It seems Chonker understand me.

Oh, how beautiful you are, dear summer morning! The sun that has just come up from behind the mountains, takes its time, steadily throwing its glance across the world, shining its gentle rays on the earth, greeting us. “Good morning, humans! How has your night been? You haven't been worried about me now, have you?”

“Of course, we have been worrying about you, dear sun! Welcome! If it wasn’t for you, we wouldn’t be happy, and we wouldn’t want to do anything.” This is how I talk to the sun. Dad and mum do not hear this inner conversation. They are silent, each having their own thoughts. As for me, I was overjoyed by all of it, taking everything in, wanting the forest we were coming out of to be left behind quickly.

Bathing in the rays of the sunrise was a morning bird. He cheerfully pours into the whole neighborhood - for sure, he enjoys life, the sun and this wonderful morning. Great Father had some name for it... Hang on! Wait! Yes, I remember: 'nʕaevla' (нІаьвла), the lark. This is the morning singer. He meets everybody who gets up early. We are getting close to the mountain slope. The road meanders through a forest ascending onto a mountain ridge. My parents have gotten off the wagon and are continuing on foot.

“Let the reins go, Dakaz. The horse knows exactly where it should go,” says dad, padding her on the back.

Of course, I also know very well where we are heading. I have explored every corner of the forest clearings with my friends. We come here in spring, summer and fall. We get to eat forest strawberries, try the fruits on the clearings, play here and try to swing from the wild vine branches growing in the trees.

In times gone by, people from the villages around used to live here, make clearings and work their fields. That was a heavy job, since they had to cut down the trees with axes, remove the stumps, and clear the surface where they wanted to plant their crops. Some of them had lived in these clearings for many years, and when they needed anything, they would go down to the village below.

Great Father and our grandma told us stories that after the Chechens were taken away from their homeland to Siberia, cattle and dogs were roaming these clearings. People were forced to leave everything they had when they were deported.

No one lived there afterwards. The authorities take special care of the forests and the clearings. Nowadays people ask permission from the forester to cut grass here for making hay. They also need to buy a token if they want to collect wood for the winter.

Finally, we get to the clearing. It is not very far, once you get on top of the mountain ridge. The forest surrounds it from all sides, and it is quite big.

“Dad, are we going to mow all of this?” I ask, pointing into the direction of the clearing.

“No, Dakaz, we will only mow half of it. Our neighbour Salman and I have agreed to share it this year.”

Together we unharness Chonker, take off the fetters from her legs, and let her graze on a small meadow on the other side of the road. He has plenty of fresh grass to eat over there. Dad takes two scythes from the wagon and puts them down on the ground. The scythes’ blades radiate in the sun. Dad had sharpened and prepared them with special care.

“Let’s eat something before we start. Get here both of you,” says mum, who has spread out a blanket with a jar of sour cream, handmade flatbread, bread from the shop, green unions, cucumbers, tomatoes, boiled potatoes and red radishes.

We eat something. It seems to be particularly nice sitting here like this, early in the morning in a forest. While mum is stuffing us with food she eats very little herself. She tries to get dad and me to eat more.

“Come on and eat something, don’t hold back. You’ve come here to work,” dad tells her.

“But I am eating.” Mum picks up something before she is told twice.

I notice that dad and mum hold their left hand under their mouth as they eat, so as not to spill bread.

We quickly finish eating and get up together.

“Don’t try to keep up with me, woman! You should keep to your own pace,” says dad as he picks up his scythe and goes to the clearing.

“I won’t try to keep up with you,” says mum, looking at me with a wink. “I’m no match for you.”

Without saying anything else, dad starts mowing. He holds the scythe comfortably, as if it were a toy. The regular rustle of mowed grass follows. “Ssshh, ssshh, ssshh.”

Soon mum starts after him. Her swing is not so wide, but she works a little bit faster.

‎‎I do not get a scythe just yet, but I am slowly learning. As soon as mum gets tired, I will show her what I am capable of, and let her have a break.

‎‎The grass is tall and thick. The night's dew has not yet evaporated.

I am sitting under the huge tree where we had our breakfast. Dad tasked me to keep an eye on Chonker, to make sure he would not wander away too far.

Well, I am sure he will not wander off. There is plenty of grass where he is at the other side of the road. Look how carefully he is eating this mountain grass. As if, it has a special taste.

As I sit under the tree and keep an eye on what my parents are doing, I start thinking: “What should I be doing?” When we get home, I should have something to tell my friends. Something that I saw or did. Bakar might tease me. “Really? What a huge event for you to accompany your dad to cut hay…”

What I am going to tell him better be something special, something great. Something he has not seen or heard yet.

The sun is really getting higher now, but the temperature is still quite bearable. My brave parents are mowing the hay at a quick pace. Every now and then, you can hear the 'shirk, shirk' of the whetstone against iron, as dad sharpens the scythe. He uses his thumb to check how sharp the blade is. When dad gets close to mum, he sometimes stops and sharpens her scythe too.

“Dak-az!” mum’s loud voice sounds from afar.

“Daka-az! Daka-az!” the echo answers from somewhere deep inside the mountain. This continues to amaze me. Whenever my friends and I come here to the forest, we have lots of fun shouting and listening to the echoed responses. ‎‎They say the echoes are devils and they are shouting because they want to cut out our hearts.

“Daka-az! Please bring the jug of milk,” mum shouts one more time.

“Coming up!” I shout back. The clay jug is standing under the tree. I cover it with some cloth and pick it up. Our voices have mixed completely by now, and the echoes are utterly incomprehensible. “Whatever! I'll get it!” I say. I get the jug, and turn to go to the gorge.

“Right, what do you say to that, enemies of God?” I am talking to the devils now. No idea whether they hear me or not. Anyway, they did not get to do whatever they wanted to.

“Come on! Let your dad drink first.” The sweat is running down mum’s face. It looks as if her face was splashed with water. She takes off her white headscarf, wipes her face, and carefully puts it back on. I go to dad.

“Are you keeping an eye on the horse?” he asks, and then drinks from the jug.

“He was grazing where we took him,” I answer. I am thinking, “Where would he go anyway?”

The milk leaves white traces on dad's black moustache. I involuntarily laugh, when I see the white traces on his moustache.

“What's so funny, you son of a devil?” Dad pretends to be angry. He knows why I am laughing. He wipes the milk from his moustache and smiles broadly too. Mum stands nearby and watching what we are doing. She is smiling too.

I take her some milk. “Have you drunk something, Dakaz?” She hands me back the jug.

“No, I don’t want to. I’ll drink some water if I’m thirsty. Nearby in the gorge at the other side there’s a spring with very cold water.”

“Okay, at lunch break you and I will go there to fetch water.”

“Oh, that place… I can go there myself. It’s not that far from here.”

“My dear little Dakaz, mum hugs me, “how could I send you into the woods by yourself just like that?”

“Let me go, let go!” I try to get out of my mum’s arms. “If dad sees, it won't be nice. You continue to treat me like a baby.”

Mum starts laughing. “Off you go then. I’ve utterly embarrassed you,” says mum as she gives me one last pat. “You’ll always be my little boy, even in forty years’ time.”

“Mum, will you let me cut some grass?” I ask as I pick up the scythe.

“Give it a try, then. Show what you’re capable of. Do you remember what I taught you?”

“Sure I do.” I sweep the scythe to the side.

I am not very good at it. Either the scythe strikes the ground, or it swings up too high, not cutting any grass at all. Mom takes the scythe and shows me how to do it once more. Now I am getting a little better at it. I mow a few meters without making a mistake. While dad is sharpening his scythe again, he watches me.

“Don’t jerk the scythe. Sweep it evenly. Don’t sweep with your arms,” he says. “No problem, you’ll be better at it next year,” he adds.

Soon after, mum takes over.

“Dakaz, go look under the tree, maybe ants have found our food. Once we've finished these few rows, dad and I will come.”

She says so, because she thinks I am tired. I will not quarrel with mum although I am able to pick up the scythe again. I start back to the pear tree. The stubs of cut grass prick the soles of my bare feet. The grass is not pricking, but rather tickling my feet as I walk on it. As for the smell – it is just sensational. Now ‎‎I am passing through the uncut knee-high grass. There are blue, yellow and red flowers. They look so beautiful in between the green grass. Great Father knows all the names of these herbs and flowers. He told me their names when we came here together.

‎‎Back at the pear tree, I sit down under it. I get to the canister with the rest of the milk and cover it with a cloth again. I am still warm from the cutting, so I should not drink cold water or I would catch a cold. Granny says it’s better to drink boiled milk for thirst. That is why mum and dad drink milk during the mowing. I do not particularly care for milk... All of a sudden, I hear a bird calling from the bushes, “Hoop-poe! Hoo-poe!” Then again, “Hoo-poe! Hoo-poe!” It continues like this for some time. Dad says this is the hoopoe. I am familiar with the calls of some birds. This is a particularly distinctive one. I sit there transfixed, listening to the hoopoe’s call. Its sound is peculiar. It is a bit sad, as if the bird is sharing a tragedy…

The time goes by as I sit there, listening to the hoopoe. Then it stops as abruptly, as if it has never made any sound. Right above my head a butterfly is fluttering with huge wings. “Wow, it is so pretty. I need to catch it and take it to Malika, my sister. She’ll get really excited when she sees that I’ve brought her such a beautiful butterfly from the woods.” I love my little sister. She is almost five now, and she is very gentle and quiet. Whatever she picks up, she talks to and plays with.

I start after the butterfly. It manages to escape me for quite some time. As soon as I think I caught it, it flutters its wings and flies off again. It takes me a long time, but then I catch it. I tell it, “I finally got you!” With the utmost care, I put it under my cap, because I do not want to crush its wings.

Mum and dad will come at lunchtime. I take the metal bucket with its lid and set out for the spring. As one gets over the ridge, there is a gorge with a very good spring. Its water is really cold, fresh and clean. There are several ancient stones at the fountain of the spring, and there's also an iron pipe here. Someone has put these here to clean the spring and take care of it. The mouth of the spring is also clean. There is no mud nor anything lying around.

Great Father and dad always tell us to take good care of water places. We need to treat them with care. Near the spring, vegetation like black elderberries and some kind of herb with wide leaves grow. When we came here before, we would twist one of those long leaves to make some kind of cup and drink water from it, even though there a real cup would always be present at the fountain of the spring. My friends enjoyed drinking from the leaf, though I am not sure why they chose to drink that way. Was it to practice drinking from a grass cup or was there some other reason? Then, when we had drunk enough, we would splash each another.

I hear mum calling me. I pick up my bucket filled to the brim, and hop back up onto the ridge. There are dry leaves under the trees that have fallen down in previous years. They are soft. My feet slip as I ascend.

“Why have you gone through the forest without anyone nearby?” mum asks me disapprovingly, as I get to the pear tree. “If you had stumbled upon a wild animal, what would you have done?”

Dad comes to my aid. “Please stop! Where are the wild animals here? They run off far away, as soon as they hear human voices. Don’t worry, it’s okay for the boy to walk here, he’ll learn to be brave.”

“What you say turns the boy into a savage”, says mum. She takes the water I brought and starts rinsing the cutlery.

I stand there, waiting silently. I feel guilty. I know that I have upset mum.

We have lunch and eat whatever is left from this morning.

When the noon heat has passed its peak, dad and mum start to cut hay again. When mum gets tired, I do what I am able, but my hay cutting is not half as good as theirs.

It is getting evening now. Dad and mum managed to cover a large area.

Dad says, “We will let the hay dry for a couple of days, gather it, bind sheaves, and then bring it home.”

Great Father always says, “He who doesn’t use his brain in summer, will not have a kettle of food in winter.” I understand this saying quite well now.
